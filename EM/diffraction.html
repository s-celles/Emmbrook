Light scattering from a subwavelength array

Assume that there are \( N \) scatterers, uniformly spaced by \( a \), with total length \( (N-1) a \): a finite lattice. The lattice could be a diffraction grating made by optical lithography (probed by visible light, like a laser pointer) or a crystal lattice (probed by x-rays or electron waves, as in a transmission electron microscope.)

The simulation assumes that each scatterer is a point source for a spherical light wave, radiating outwards from the point.)

You will see that the diffraction pattern images: the Fourier transform of the lattice. Since atomic spacings (periodicities) are characteristic of elements, this allows us to determine a material's composition.

Consider the wave front on the screen is $$ \begin{equation} \begin{split} \tilde{E} &= \frac{ a }{ r } \exp(i k r) \\ &= \frac{ a }{ \sqrt{(x - x')^2 + y'^2 + z^2} } \thinspace \exp \Big( i k \sqrt{(x - x')^2 + y'^2 + z^2} \Big), \end{split} \end{equation} $$ where \( (x', y') \) is the position of the image point, \( (x,0) \) is the position of the scatterer. The intensity is \( \tilde{E} \tilde{E}^\ast \).

If \( x^2 \ll z^2 \), or \( x'^2, y'^2 \ll z^2 \), we call that they satisfy paraxial approximation. If \( x^2 / \lambda \ll z \), or \( x'^2 / \lambda, y'^2 / \lambda \ll z \), we call that they satisfy far field approximation, where \( x^2 / (\lambda z) \) is called Fresnel number.
