EM polarization

The \( \mathbf{E} \) field vector and \( \mathbf{B} \) field vector of an electromagnetic wave form a plane perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation, i.e., \( \hat{\mathbf{ k }} \). In the mean time, \( \mathbf{E} \) field vector and \( \mathbf{B} \) field vector are also perpendicular to each other. Here we assume $$ \begin{align} E_x = E &= \sin ( \mathbf{ k } \cdot \mathbf{ z } - \omega t ), \\ B_y = B &= \sin ( \mathbf{ k } \cdot \mathbf{ z } - \omega t + \phi ), \\ \end{align} $$ i.e., there is a phase difference \( \phi \) between E- and B-field. If \( \phi = 0 \), the EM wave is called linear-polarized or plane-polarized. If \( \phi = \pm \pi / 2 \), the EM wave is called circular-polarized, otherwise it is called elliptic-polarized.
